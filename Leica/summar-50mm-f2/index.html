    Leica Summar 50mm f/2

    The Summar was Leica's first f/2 standard lens, produced from 1933 to 1940. It is a collapsible lens on the Leica Thread Mount (LTM/M39), and one of the most characterful lenses of the era — rendering with soft contrast, gentle falloff, and a distinct glow wide open. Nearly all surviving examples have some degree of haze; a clean one is rare.

    Service is rewarding but requires patience. The glass is uncoated and extremely soft. "Summar haze" — a cloudiness within cemented element groups — is common and cannot be resolved by cleaning alone.

    Tools & Materials

    • Spanner wrench (adjustable, fine-tipped for small rings)
    • Rubber friction grip
    • JIS screwdrivers: #00, #0
    • 99% isopropyl alcohol
    • Lighter fluid (Ronsonol) — for grease removal
    • Lens tissue and Eclipse fluid (or distilled water with a drop of dish soap)
    • Cotton gloves — mandatory; the uncoated glass shows every fingerprint
    • Soft cotton swabs
    • Helicoid grease (light viscosity — Molykote EM-30L or similar)
    • Loupe (10x minimum)

    Before You Start

    Summar haze

    The most common issue — a milky or oily cloudiness between the cemented rear elements — is not removable by surface cleaning. It is internal cement degradation. If present, the only remedy is professional re-cementing or sourcing replacement elements. Confirm whether haze is on a surface (cleanable) or internal (not) before disassembling.

    Uncoated glass

    The Summar's elements have no coating. The glass scratches extremely easily — far more so than modern coated lenses. Always use generous fluid and zero pressure. Never use dry tissue. One overzealous wipe can permanently damage an element.

    Service Steps

    Step 1: Assess the lens

    Extend the lens fully and hold it up to a bright light source. Look through from both ends at various angles. Map what you find:

    • Surface haze (on front or rear element face) — cleanable
    • Internal haze within a cemented group — not cleanable
    • Fungus — may be cleanable if caught early; etching is permanent
    • Oil on aperture blades — cleanable

    Rack focus and feel for stiffness or gritty spots in the helicoid.

    Step 2: Remove the focus scale ring

    The knurled focus scale ring at the base of the lens barrel is retained by two or three small set screws. Loosen them (they may be under a lacquer seal — break it gently with a scalpel). Slide the ring off.

    Step 3: Remove the front element group

    With the lens fully extended, the front element group unscrews counterclockwise. Use a rubber grip for friction. It may be tight from old lacquer or hardened grease. Once loose it will unthread smoothly.

    Set it aside face-up on a clean surface.

    Mark the thread position

    Before removing, mark the barrel and front group with a felt marker so you can return it to the same rotational position. This preserves focus calibration.

    Step 4: Inspect and clean aperture blades

    With the front group removed, the aperture blades are accessible. Use a loupe to check for oil. Oily blades appear shiny or stick when actuated.

    If oily: dip a cotton swab barely in lighter fluid and gently wipe each blade. Let dry completely before proceeding. Blades should be uniformly matte black.

    Step 5: Remove the rear element group

    The rear element group unscrews from the back of the barrel. Access it from the rear (camera-mount side). Use a spanner or rubber grip. Turn counterclockwise.

    Handle with extreme care

    The rear group contains the cemented elements most prone to Summar haze. Do not apply pressure to the glass. Hold by the metal cell only.

    Step 6: Clean the elements

    Clean each exposed glass surface with lens tissue and a drop of Eclipse fluid. Use minimal pressure — let the fluid do the work. Inspect with a loupe after each pass.

    For stubborn haze on a surface (not internal), a dilute solution of distilled water and a single drop of dish soap can help. Rinse with distilled water, then finish with Eclipse.

    Do not clean internal cement surfaces

    If a cemented group has internal haze, do not attempt to separate the elements. This requires optical cement and a UV curing lamp. Leave it to a specialist or accept it as-is.

    Step 7: Clean and regrease the helicoid

    With both element groups removed, the helicoid is accessible. Clean old grease from the threads using lighter fluid on a swab. The original grease in these lenses is 80+ years old and will have turned waxy or gummy.

    Apply a thin coat of fresh light helicoid grease. The Summar's helicoid is short — a light touch of grease is sufficient.

    Step 8: Reassemble

    Reinstall the rear element group first, threading it in clockwise. Then reinstall the front group, aligning to your earlier mark if you made one.

    Reinstall the focus scale ring, align the infinity mark, and tighten the set screws.

    Step 9: Verify

    • [ ] Aperture blades are clean and snap cleanly through all stops
    • [ ] Focus ring moves smoothly with light, even damping through full travel
    • [ ] No haze, smears, or debris visible on any element surface
    • [ ] Lens collapses and extends without binding
    • [ ] Infinity focus is accurate on a distant subject

    Notes

    • Chrome and black-paint versions exist; the black-paint examples are early and rarer. Service is identical.
    • The non-standard minimum aperture of f/12.5 is characteristic and correct — not a miscalibration.
    • A hazy Summar still produces usable, often beautiful images. Many photographers prefer the soft wide-open rendering that mild internal haze can enhance. Don't over-service a lens that's producing results you like.
    • The Summar was succeeded by the Summitar (1939) and later the Summicron. Summitar service is similar.
